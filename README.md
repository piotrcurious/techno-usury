# techno-usury
collection of essays on technological debt and usury 

Preface

George Orwell’s cold observation about mass psychology — “Whereas Socialism, and even capitalism in a more grudging way, have said to people ‘I offer you a good time,’ Adolf Hitler has said to them, ‘I offer you struggle, danger, and death,’ and as a result a whole nation flings itself at his feet” — is more than a historical diagnosis of totalitarian appeal. It is a compact map of how a certain emotional grammar works: promises that foreground struggle, identity and the illusion of meaning can overwhelm sober calculation and invite collective submission. 

In our conversation we traced the modern analogue of that grammar into everyday life: the technical spectacle of the automobile. High-technology cars sell a vision — prestige, capability, the thrill of novelty — while simultaneously embedding a quiet ledger of future burdens (proprietary modules, locked diagnostics, expensive calibrations). When those burdens arrive they are felt as threats to the owner’s sense of investment and identity; the combination of prior spending, aspirational attachment and an offer of easy fixes produces a behavioural pattern that closely resembles the anthropological “cargo-cult” response — ritual imitation and faith in symbols rather than in structural remedies. The cargo-cult literature shows these movements arose from desperate inequality and the attraction of apparent, miraculous goods; the metaphor fits when societies embrace technical spectacle without sufficient attention to underlying systems. 

That psychological and cultural alignment becomes economically toxic when two material facts are added: debt is real and cheap credit lubricates commitment. German households today hold very large financial positions and face rising pockets of over-indebtedness even as many keep substantial exposure to durable consumption — a context documented by the national banking data and recent surveys. Those macro and micro balance-sheet realities make it far easier to buy into the charm of high-tech consumption and far harder to walk away when the bill arrives. In other words, when ownership is front-loaded with prestige and back-loaded with technical debt, inexpensive finance and opaque replacement markets conspire to convert aesthetic desire into long-term economic drag. 

This is not a mere metaphor. Economic research on household indebtedness shows a common pattern: increased borrowing can lift consumption and growth in the short run but tends to leave economies more fragile and slower-growing in the medium run; high household debt ratios are associated with weaker output and greater sensitivity to shocks. Institutional analyses reach the same conclusion: the easier the credit and the more deferred the true cost, the more likely societies are to accumulate systemic liabilities that reduce future prosperity. Put plainly — the charm of the gadget, amplified by the ritual of brand and status, compounds with leverage until what once felt like progress becomes structural drag. 

So the danger we highlight in these conclusions is twofold and mutually reinforcing. First: cultural narratives of struggle, status and authenticity — whether politically framed or commercially engineered — can short-circuit prudential judgement and encourage collective commitment to costly, brittle infrastructures. Second: financial structures that make those commitments painless today (cheap credit, easy refinancing, opaque after-markets) ensure that the commitments become long-lasting liabilities tomorrow. The result is an irresistible, inescapable charm that extracts labor, savings and resilience from households and funnels them into supplier chains and service rents — a transfer that damages livelihoods and, at scale, weakens national economic performance. 

If Orwell’s line warns us about the emotional architecture of dangerous politics, then our empirical account warns about the emotional-economic architecture of modern consumption. Both rely on a furnace of feeling — promised meaning, deferred cost, and easy access to credit — that converts personal choices into collective loss. The policy and personal remedies therefore must match that structure: reduce the glamour-to-debt pipeline (design for repairability, transparent total cost signals), tighten the financial channels that make deferred cost seductive (smarter credit terms and consumer education), and rebuild collective alternatives (public mobility and infrastructure that provide functional dignity without binding households to expensive technical debt). Only by confronting both the cultural script and the economic mechanics can a society cut the spell and redirect resources toward durable public goods rather than toward ritualised private consumption.

(Sources cited: Orwell’s commentary; scholarship on cargo cult movements; Bundesbank and national surveys on household financial positions and over-indebtedness; BIS/IMF research on the medium-term effects of household debt and fragility.)
