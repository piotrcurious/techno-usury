Extended Preface

As George Orwell observed about the emotional grammar that powers mass movements — captured in his comment on Mein Kampf — the promise of drama and identity outcompetes offers of mere comfort. That psychological architecture helps explain why technologically enchanted consumption (the smart car, the glossy app, the seamless credit offer) becomes less a rational purchase and more a ritual — and why those rituals can be engineered, monetized and weaponized by powerful interests.

Techno-usury gods (the altar of easy credit and expensive tech). Cheap, widely available credit + opaque product ecosystems create what I call techno-usury: deferred payments that make sophisticated, fragile devices feel affordable today while funneling future rents to manufacturers, financiers and service chains. Macro signals from institutions such as Bundesbank and the BIS show how leverage and consumer credit amplify short-run consumption but increase systemic fragility. In plain language: finance lubricates charm, and charm manufactures long-term obligation.

Cognitive dissonance and the ritual of upgrade. Cognitive dissonance — the mental tension between “I paid a lot” and “this keeps breaking / costing more” — pushes owners to rationalize more spending. Brand mythology and social signalling convert small technical advantages into markers of status; the owner then rewrites experience (“it’s worth it”) to eliminate discomfort. Consumer advocates (for example, clubs like ADAC) repeatedly document how a single sensor failure or calibration can cascade into thousands of euros of repairs — the perfect trigger for dissonance-driven escalation.

Who profits from collective narcissism. The architecture of profitable spectacle depends on collective narcissism: a culture that treats possessions as extensions of identity. Vehicle manufacturers, large suppliers and dealer networks (represented in industry groups such as VDA) benefit in three linked ways:

1. they sell status and novelty at premium margins;


2. they design products that are expensive to service or replace (capturing future aftermarket rents); and


3. they rely on financial products that keep consumers locked in while rents are extracted downstream. The more an economy equates personal worth with the latest technology, the wider the rent-capture becomes.



How severity of debt + ease of drawing it compounds charm. Two forces compound: (a) the severity of technological debt (when a single module failure costs as much as a year’s rent) and (b) the ease of acquiring credit or other short-term fixes. Together they create a trap: the upfront glamour lures the buyer; credit smooths the pain; rising maintenance and replacement obligations become routine — and the owner becomes a recurring revenue stream rather than a customer who is served. At scale, this diverts household savings into supplier profits, reduces resilience, and concentrates economic risk.

Breaking the spell — how not to be a flying monkey of the system. The metaphor “flying monkeys” fits: many people do the work of advertising, signalling and sustaining systems that ultimately extract their resources. The antidote is both personal and collective.

Personal moves (practical, immediate):

Commit to total-cost shopping: compare lifetime costs (purchase + predictable maintenance + likely module replacements) before buying.

Use a strict repair vs replace rule: if a repair exceeds ~40–50% of market value, seriously prefer replacement or downgrade. (Get two independent quotes.)

Pre-commit budgets: set an annual repair ceiling; if a quote exceeds it, sell or replace.

Favor products with repairability, standard parts, and independent-garage support — or buy older, simpler models intentionally (the Cuore archetype we discussed).

Move savings toward low-risk buffers (emergency fund) to reduce reliance on credit.


Collective & political moves (systemic leverage):

Demand right-to-repair and diagnostic openness so independent shops can compete and parts markets remain competitive.

Support public-transport investment and community mobility (reducing the cultural prestige premium of cars).

Back regulatory moves that require transparent lifetime cost labelling (finance + maintenance) and curb exploitative consumer credit terms.

Organize co-ops and local repair networks to scale alternatives to dealer-centric servicing and to keep skills in the community.


Cultural moves (change the story):

Reframe status away from “newest gadget” to “durable, maintainable, and community-useful.” Highlight stories of people who chose robustness and won back time and money.

Expose the rent structure: illustrate who gains when a sensor is sealed in a bumper and who pays when it fails. Knowledge breaks glamour.


Final practical thought. The machinery that converts desire into long-term extraction is not mystical — it’s engineered with product design, financial architecture and cultural marketing. If Orwell’s warning is about the emotional grammar that conquers judgment, then our task is to rewire incentives, information and social narratives so that the obvious pleasures of novelty do not become the slow furnace that burns personal resilience and public wealth. Break the ritual: demand transparency, insist on repairability, resist financing that hides long-term cost, and invest in community alternatives. That is how a society stops being complicit and starts reclaiming its savings and its future.
