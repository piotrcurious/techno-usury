Extra chapter — Cyberpunk engineers of techno-usury

In a world where prestige is engineered as product, there are two kinds of makers: those who build useful tools, and those who craft durable rituals of desire. The cyberpunk engineers of techno-usury belong to the second tribe: designers, financiers and storytellers who fuse flashy capability with locked-down replace-not-repair architectures, then smooth purchase with credit and maintain dependence through opaque after-markets. Their economy is not merely built on parts and labour — it’s built on captured future cash flows.

Flagship personalities and cultural legitimation

Every large cultural project needs a public face. Celebrated engineers and brand founders become shorthand for mastery and progress; their biographies and mythic status help normalize complex systems and obscure their long-term costs. Think of figures like Ferdinand Porsche — a real historical example of how an individual can personify an industry’s promise and style, helping a technology become an object of common aspiration. 

That public legitimacy matters. When a charismatic figure or a glossy brand promises “safety, status, and the future,” people are primed to accept trade-offs that look technical and inevitable — expensive closed modules, proprietary diagnostics, and subscription services — rather than political or economic choices.

Where the profit streams flow (the invisible ledger)

The money extracted from households does not vanish; it concentrates into particular pockets:

Service and dealer margins. The German motor trade’s service segment is large — workshops and dealer servicing together generate substantial recurring turnover, feeding margins across chains and brands. 

Aftermarket & Tier-1 supplier capture. Large suppliers and module specialists capture R&D rents and high-margin parts sales — a structural transfer from dispersed consumers to concentrated industrial actors. 

Labour-time monetization. Rising workshop hourly rates multiply the impact of complexity: more calibration, more diagnostic hours, more billable time. 

Finance & interest. Credit, leasing and refinancing turn future income into today’s purchases; the household pays interest and occasionally slips toward over-indebtedness, which benefits lenders and keeps the consumption engine running. 


These are the modern routes of rent extraction: not theft in a single act, but long, legal flows that convert daily life into steady returns for capital.

The hidden persuaders: techniques that manufacture consent

The ways people are persuaded to enter and to stay in techno-usury are neither mystical nor accidental — they are engineered. Vance Packard’s classic diagnosis of psychological manipulation still reads as a blueprint for modern persuasion: advertising, motivation research and symbolic marketing shape wants and justify consumption. The Hidden Persuaders taught us to look for these tools; today they are embedded in product launches, UX design and finance offers. 

Concretely, the “hidden persuaders” in this sector include:

Storytelling that ties identity to ownership (ads, influencers, lifestyle marketing).

Interface design that privileges upgrades and subscriptions (software nudges, “feature locked behind paywall”).

Finance framing that presents expensive purchases as tiny monthly slices (leasing, deferred payment).

Product framing that disguises replace-not-repair choices as “safety” or “service improvements.”
Each device reduces friction for an initial purchase and raises friction for exit.


John Frum, cargo cults, and the engineering of hope

The John Frum movement on Tanna — the archetypal cargo cult — is a powerful analogy: followers performed rituals to summon material abundance because they had seen seemingly miraculous supplies arrive during wartime. John Frum and his story show how visible abundance can create durable faith in symbolic practices that lack structural causal power. 

In consumer tech, the “cargo” is capability (autonomy, connectivity, prestige). The rituals are purchases, firmware updates, dealer visits and social signalling. Like the cult, the social mechanism thrives on visible tokens rather than systemic provisioning: polished dashboards, branded lobbies, and glowing ads substitute for transparent costs and durable public goods. That sympathetic magic channels hope into market demand and, crucially, into predictable revenue streams for the techno-usury ecosystem.

Who benefits — and who is made vulnerable

The diaspora of profit flows makes clear who gains:

Large suppliers and platform owners profit when hardware becomes software-capped and when modules must be replaced rather than serviced. 

Dealerships and certified service networks benefit from proprietary tools and limited diagnostic access. 

Financial intermediaries collect interest and fees as consumers smooth payments or refinance. 

Marketing systems and reputational architects (agencies, celebrity endorsers, brand strategists) profit by keeping desire and identity aligned with consumption. The methods Packard described remain profitable because they work. 


Those made vulnerable are precisely the people the system seduces: households with constrained budgets, social pressure to display status, and limited access to independent technical knowledge. When things go wrong — a failed sensor, a needed calibration, a surprise software update behind a paywall — the bills compound quickly, and the household balance sheet weakens even as headline GDP shows activity.

How to spot the signature moves (a short checklist)

1. Subscriptioning of functionality. If a feature that used to be a hardware capability is now locked behind a recurring fee, you’re in techno-usury territory.


2. Opaque diagnostic chains. If only brand-authorized channels can diagnose or reset systems, expect higher service margins.


3. Replace-not-repair design. If a single small part failure forces a full module swap and calibration, the design privileges revenues over repairability.


4. Finance normalization. If the default purchase path is leasing or long-term financing that obscures total cost, the product is being sold as a ritual, not as a durable tool.


5. Personality legitimation. When a charismatic leader or founder becomes the shorthand for product trust, check whether that cult of personality masks economic lock-in. (Historical iconography like that surrounding figures such as Ferdinand Porsche shows how engineering charisma can create durable myths.) 



A short roadmap to defensive practice

Demand right-to-repair and diagnostic access so independent workshops and consumers can choose lower-cost paths. (This breaks dealer monopolies on calibration revenue.)

Require clear lifetime cost labelling at point of sale (purchase price, expected calibration/diagnostic costs, subscription exposure). Transparency reduces persuasion’s weaponry.

Limit predatory financing through consumer protection rules that highlight effective interest rates and long-run total costs. Central banks and regulators play an essential role here. 

Support public narratives of sufficiency — celebrate repairability and long-life designs in media so prestige decouples from churnable novelty. Packard’s diagnosis still points the way: we must see and critique the emotional engineering.

Closing image

Imagine a runway lit not to summon planes, but to make a village convinced the lights themselves carry abundance. The cyberpunk engineers of techno-usury design brighter lights, hire the showmen and sell longer tickets to the spectacle. The policy task and civic work is to dismantle the stage: reclaim diagnostic tools, democratise repair skills, regulate finance, and change the stories we tell about progress. Only then will the cargo stop arriving as spectacle and start arriving as systems that serve real needs.
