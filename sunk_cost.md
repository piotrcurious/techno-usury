What the bias is (short)

The sunk-cost fallacy is the tendency to let past, irrecoverable investments (money, time, status) affect a present decision that should be forward-looking. In cars this shows up as continuing to repair or “invest” in an asset even when replacing it would be cheaper or safer. The phenomenon is well documented in behavioral economics. Daniel Kahneman and classic papers by Arkes & Blumer formalised the psychology; field work shows sunk costs affect car usage and replacement decisions. 

Why technological debt makes sunk-cost mistakes bigger (mechanics → psychology)

1. High up-front purchase & upgrade investment — modern high-tech cars (ADAS, many ECUs, expensive modules) have large sunk costs in purchase price, fitted options and dealer-only servicing. That makes owners feel they “must” protect that investment. (Example German monthly TCO studies put modern small/tech cars well above low-tech used cars.) 


2. Complex, vendor-locked repairs — when a tiny collision means an OEM module + calibration (expensive, dealer-only), the next invoice is large and salient; owners anchor on what they’ve already spent and keep authorizing repairs. 


3. Uncertainty & optimism — people over-optimistically assume that “next month” the car will be fine after a repair, or resale value will recover what they spend (optimism & loss-aversion interact). Kahneman/Tversky framing research explains this mental accounting. 


4. Liquidity & financing framing — financed buyers rationalize continuing repairs because the monthly loan payment must be paid anyway; the psychological framing makes the marginal repair feel “free” relative to the loan. (This is a framing/mental-accounting trap rather than sound economics.)

Common rationalizations and the reality checks (debunking)

Below are the most frequent rationalizations owners give — and a short, factual rebuttal + a concrete check.

1. “I’ll recoup this at resale — I can’t sell for a fair price otherwise.”
Reality check: depreciation is a forward loss, not reversible by extra repairs. For many modern cars the market value falls faster than repair spending — you rarely recover large repair bills in resale unless repairs correct a defect that otherwise makes the car unsellable. Use a fresh market valuation (mobile.de / DAT / AutoUncle) before authorizing major work: if the repair cost > ~40–50% of current market value, replacement is usually the better option. (Rule-of-thumb and dealer guidance widely report the ~50% threshold). 


2. “I’ve financed this car; the monthly payment forces me to keep it.”
Reality check: financing is a liquidity constraint, not an economic justification for further sunk spending. Compute total monthly cost of (a) keeping + repairing vs (b) selling/voluntary termination / trading in and paying the difference or switching to a cheaper car subscription. In Germany there are alternative ownership models (subscriptions) that sometimes beat continued heavy repair bills. Ask your lender about voluntary termination or trade-in penalties — the correct decision depends on the net present cost, not emotions. 


3. “I already spent on dealer servicing — only dealer can maintain warranty/quality.”
Reality check: warranty math matters only if warranty coverage still applies and actually covers the failure. For most out-of-warranty electronic/module failures, dealers add premium labour and OEM parts; independent garages plus aftermarket parts are often cheaper and sufficient. Get a second quote and compare total cost (parts + labour + recalibration) before acquiescing to the highest quote. ADAC analyses repeatedly show wide price spreads between providers. 


4. “This car has special features I need — I can’t get that in a cheaper car.”
Reality check: owners overvalue features they already possess (endowment effect). Ask: which features do you actually use daily? If ADAS or infotainment is rarely used, its replacement cost is pure extra. Frame the decision as “which functions do I need for commute/safety?” and compare to cheaper models that meet those needs. (Behavioral research: endowment & status-quo biases push people to keep possessions.) 


5. “Fixing it now will avoid a bigger bill later.”
Reality check: this can be true when routine maintenance prevents catastrophic failure (e.g., timing belt), but it’s false when the repair is for a recurring, high-cost electronic module where the next failure is independent of current work. Distinguish preventive, value-creating maintenance (keep) from “symptom repair” on expensive modules that have low salvage value (consider replace). Use inspection reports (TÜV/DEKRA) and a trusted diagnosis to classify the work. 


6. “I’m emotionally attached — I’ve had it for years.”
Reality check: emotional attachment is normal (endowment + loss aversion). Make one quantitative step: write down the last 3 repair invoices and the car’s current market value. If cumulative recent repairs + expected near-term repairs > 50% of current value, emotion is the only reason to keep paying — and that’s not an economic justification.



A short decision checklist to defeat sunk-cost bias (practical)

1. Market-value first: obtain a current private-sale value (DAT / mobile.de / AutoUncle).


2. One-line math: compute NetRepairCost = Quote + expected follow-up costs and ReplacementCost = (price of acceptable replacement) − (expected sale/trade-in value). Replace if NetRepairCost > ReplacementCost. (If you want a quick rule: if repair > ~50% of market value, seriously consider replacing.) 


3. Safety filter: always prioritise safety-critical repairs (brakes, steering, tyres), even if borderline on price. Don’t gamble on safety to avoid sunk costs. (TÜV data show brakes/tyres are common HU failures — safety matters.) 


4. Two independent quotes: get at least one independent garage quote (not dealer) and a used-car valuer (DAT or similar). ADAC and independent studies show big provider price spreads. 


5. Account for alternatives: factor in subscriptions, leasing or a simple used replacement (often cheaper monthly TCO than repeated high repairs). 


6. Pre-commit rule: set a fixed annual repair budget (e.g., €X/year) beyond which you replace; this removes ad-hoc escalation decisions.

Quick example (illustrative numbers)

Car market value today = €6,000. Dealer quote to fix ADAS + module + calibration = €3,500. Replacement (similar used car) = €7,500 with trade-in of the current car = €5,000 → net replacement cost = €2,500.
→ Repair (€3,500) > Replacement (€2,500): replace. But the owner says “I already paid €20k for it” — that’s sunk cost and shouldn’t matter to the forward decision.


Final practical tips (behavioural nudges)

Reframe the decision in future-only terms: “If I sold the car today, would I pay €3,500 to keep it running for the next 12 months?”

Use a cooling-off rule: if quote > threshold, wait 48 hours and get a second opinion — the emotional pressure often fades and rational choices become easier.

Use a simple spreadsheet: market value, repair quote, replacement quote, safety flag; compute which forward path is cheaper over 12–24 months.
